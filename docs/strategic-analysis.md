# BANNER COMMAND — Strategic Layer Analysis
*Design report · July 2026 · working title*

## The Core Thesis

Banner Command is built on a single design conviction that emerged in our first sessions: **the party, not the unit, is the piece**. Everything strategic in this game flows from that inversion. FF6 supplies the emotional reference — the Kefka's Tower feeling of splitting a beloved roster into teams that must each stand on their own — while Ogre Battle and Final Fantasy Tactics supply the operational grammar: squads maneuvering on a map, and intimate battlefields where a single tile matters. The game's depth comes not from any one system but from how six distinct decision layers feed consequences into each other, so that a choice made on the muster screen is still paying dividends or extracting costs ten turns into a battle.

## Layer One: The Muster (Composition)

The muster screen is the heart, and it is fundamentally an exercise in scarcity. Eleven souls, up to four parties, and every configuration is a bet about a battle you have only partial information about. The tension operates on several axes at once. Concentration versus coverage: one six-unit deathball is nearly unbeatable in a single engagement but can only be in one place, while four small parties cover the map and get individually destroyed. The speed-size rule (SPD = 6 − party size) converts this from a vague instinct into arithmetic the player can feel: mass is slow, speed is thin.

Traits turn units into muster-screen puzzle pieces rather than interchangeable stat blocks. The Centaur raises a party's speed floor, so "who gets the mount" decides which heavy squad can actually keep pace. The Ninja and Scout only receive their +2 speed in parties of two or fewer, which mechanically manufactures the recon wing — the game rewards you for fielding exactly the fragile, fast pairs that fog of war makes valuable. Healers pull double duty: modest in combat but the engine of map regeneration, so distributing two priests across four parties is a genuine dilemma. The front/back row slots add a final layer: the same six units arranged differently are a different party, and each unit's identity changes with its row (the Mage's apologetic Staff Whack versus its back-row Fire).

The Commander threads through all of it as the player's own avatar — a strong unit whose future job trees, spells, and passives will make "where do I put myself" the most personal question on the screen, especially once commander death or capture carries consequences.

## Layer Two: Deployment (Commitment)

Deployment converts the muster's abstract choices into positions, and it introduces the game's most elegant gamble: **deploy or reserve**. Every party sent to the field is offense the enemy must answer; every party held in garrison is insurance against losing the start point outright. Because an enemy who takes your start point wins immediately, an empty garrison is a standing invitation — and the AI is written to smell it. This makes the reserve decision a bet on your own aggression: field everything and you are racing to win before anything slips past you.

Fortification placement (the watchtower, sited before parties, within four tiles of home) is the first information decision of the battle — do you extend vision toward the chokepoint, cover your flank, or watch the open edge? Start point tiers (camp, fortress, castle) give this layer room to grow: stronger start points will mean more buildings, better garrison bonuses, and eventually asymmetric scenarios where one side defends a castle against a field army.

## Layer Three: Information (The Fog)

Fog of war is what makes the other layers honest. Terrain is known from the outset — commanders have maps — but living intel is not: enemy banners exist only where your vision reaches. Vision scales with party character (2 base, 3 for fast parties, 4 with a scout), which means information is *purchased at the muster screen*. A scout pair is two units not fighting anywhere, and what you buy with them is the difference between maneuvering and guessing. The watchtower converts a deployment choice into permanent map knowledge. The topbar's "foes sighted" count is deliberately unsettling: knowing three banners exist and seeing one is the game telling you your plan has unexamined assumptions.

## Layer Four: Maneuver (Position and Facing)

The battlefield rewrites classic wargame maneuver at FFT scale: moats instead of oceans, crates instead of mountain ranges, one plank crossing instead of a bridge network. Small maps make every tile decision legible — the whole engagement is visible, so position reads as intention. Parties walk their actual paths, tile by tile, around obstacles, which does more than look right: it makes flanking *watchable*, telegraphs enemy intent, and lets fog reveal mid-march.

Facing is the layer's teeth. Every move ends with a facing choice, and that choice is the turn's final tactical statement, because attacks resolve against it: frontal assaults at par, side attacks at 1.25×, back attacks at 1.5×. The result is a constant geometric subgame — you are not just moving toward enemies, you are moving toward *the tile beside or behind where they are looking*, and every facing you choose is a guess about where you'll be hit from. Fast parties become lethal not through stats but through geometry: a ninja pair that ends its move behind your hammer squad forces you to turn, and turning exposes your back to something worse.

## Layer Five: Engagement (Attrition and Stance)

Combat is deliberately continuous with the map rather than a separate world. Damage persists between clashes; parties regenerate a little each turn, more with healers, more when resting. This attrition economy creates the tempo game: a mauled squad is a countdown — catch it now or fight it healed. The defend/rest stances give stationary parties a purpose, converting "I have no good move" into doctrine: holding the plank while defending is 30% harder to break; resting behind a crate line is repairing your army mid-battle. Sieges extend the same math to structures — garrisons enjoy defensive multipliers and slow healing, so cracking a keep is a commitment measured in turns and hit points, not a doorway you walk through.

## Layer Six: Objectives (The Dual Win Condition)

Destroy every enemy banner, or take their start point. Two victory paths keep both players' maps under tension everywhere at once: you cannot simply trade armies in the center, because the flank you left open is a loss condition, not an inconvenience. The start point rules bind the whole structure together — the garrison you set at deployment is the answer to the raider you couldn't see because you skimped on scouts, who exists because of a muster decision you made before the battle began. That chain, from composition to information to geometry to survival, is the game.

## The Interlock

The layered structure works because no layer is solvable in isolation. Optimal muster depends on the map; optimal deployment depends on scouting you haven't done yet; maneuver depends on intel; engagements are won by facing set two moves earlier; objectives punish whichever layer you neglected. Future systems — commander job trees, promotions, start point abilities, scenario variety, campaign persistence — all have natural sockets in this frame, deepening individual layers without adding new ones. The foundation is sound: a small game about a big question, *where should everyone be, and which way should they face?*
